Reflections

Session 1

How can we best collect and assemble links?

Depending on what you want to do with the links, there are many ways to collect and assemble them. For example, if the links are meant to be used to navigate a website then it would make sense that they are a part of some navigation element of a page. If the links are meant to be shared as a collection of resources, perhaps a list or a table might make more sense.


How can we best describe the syntax of HTML? of CSS?

HTML is made up of a bunch of elements known as tags with various content and properties. It is the composition of these elements (tags) that ultimately create what is viewable by a user. A browser is designed specifically to interpret these tags and mark-ups according to some standard.


CSS is a way of defining the style (the look) of the elements in HTML. CSS shares some similarities with HTML syntax where the elements are ultimately described by tags. CSS also allows for a bit more fine-tuning as you can target specific elements using the built-in syntax. For example, you can target specific elements or apply style to all elements that are of the same type (tags).


What are the next instructional (and/or executional) steps based on the quality of websites produced?

Depending on the how well students are getting HTML and basic CSS, you could begin to teach students about the DESIGN aspects of Web Design. Content such as contrast, balance, usability etc can be explored and ellaborated upon. You can also assign a mini project that would ask students to prove their knowledge of basic tags and styling.



Session 2

Is there prolonged engagement in producing and ability to continue development of the website? How can you tell?


What are ways we can convey the benefit of linking on websites to ensure "complete" navigation for visitors/end-users? (viewers don't get "stuck" on a singular page)

Giving students examples of pages with broken links. For an activity, an instructor could purposely add broken links in an online scavenger hunt. Another activity could be related to the usability of a web site with broken links where students evaluate web sites based on how well they can navigate through them.


What are ways can we encourage students to consider web design with the viewer in mind?

Giving students a client from the very beginning can help give students this continuous feedback on their web pages. Some ways you can do this is to pair up the students for receiving daily feedback. Ultimately, having a client would be ideal such as a student club organization, small business or another peer.



Session 3

Any general noticings/wonderings/conclusions?



How can we foster student creativity while following industry standards?

Giving students a variety of different topics and website ideas can help foster their creativity by giving them choice and variety. Students can work on personal website ideas then also be asked to create a site for others so they understand how clients react to their design choices.



What are best practices in conveying to students development and the design process is continuous?

By giving students feedback and also allowing them to peer-grade the design of their sites, we can give students the experience of the need to iterate through their designs. Having a built-in check-in or review throughout a project's life cycle can help minimize the need for students to "redo" work since editing and drafting is happening the entire process.



Was there a progression in skills? How can you tell?

Did management over time occur as a long term developmental process (as opposed to a "one and done" development mentality)? How can you tell?
